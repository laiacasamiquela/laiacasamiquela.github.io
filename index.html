About Me


My research activities fall within the topics of stellar and galactic physics. My studies are centered on the analysis of the spectra of the stars of our Galaxy, to obtain stellar parameters (effective temperature, surface gravity, rotation, ...) and detailed chemical abundances. I obtained my PhD in physics in 2017 in the University of Barcelona, where I led the OCCASO survey. During my career I combined the results of spectroscopic analysis with those of large surveys like Gaia, to study the properties of the open cluster population of our Galaxy.


I also enjoy taking part in outreach activities such as conferences and public telescope observations. I am particularly proud of leading an outreach/educational project in the program Youth and Science for the past 6 years and counting. The project aims at encouraging scientific-technological vocations of teenagers with talent and interest in astrophysics.


I have recently made a compilation of the publicly available resources for stellar spectroscopists, including radiative transfer codes, packages for measuring atmospheric parameters and chemical abundances and linelists. You can check it out here!

Research

Open Clusters

They are gravitationally bound groups of stars formed from the same cloud of gas and dust, presumably having the same age and chemical composition. Most stars, including the Sun, are believed to be formed in stellar clusters although most of them are dissolved in the first few milion years. I have used them to trace the properties and evolution of the Galactic disk. They are also key objects to study chemical homogeneity in star formation and chemical tagging possibilities.

Reference stars for spectroscopy

Spectroscopic estimates of stellar properties are affected by inaccuracies in the input data, assumptions made in the models and by the analysis methods. I participate in the Gaia FGK Benchmark stars project which provides physical properties of a handful of FGK type stars known independently of spectroscopy.

Galactic Archaeology

”Galactic archaeologists” aim to decrypt the information locked into the chemical composition of stars coupled with their ages and kinematics, to trace the history of our Galaxy. This can be done using Gaia data together with input spectroscopic information from large spectroscopic surveys.

Participation in consortiums

I am a member of the Gaia Data Processing and Analysis Consortium (DPAC), participating in the CU8 unit. My contribution is centered on the validation of the stellar parameters calculated from the spectra and the spectrophotometry of Gaia. I also participate in the WEAVE consortium, where I contribute in the target list selection for the Galactic archaeology survey, and the calibration sample.
